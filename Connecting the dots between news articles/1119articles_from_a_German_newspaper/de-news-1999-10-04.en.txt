<H1>
Schroeder: No comment on Lafontaine book
</H1>
Chancellor Schroeder refused official comment towards criticism from
former SPD leader Oskar Lafontaine.  It would have been better if
Lafontaine had spoken with him, as well as other SPD and Green
politicians, personally, said Schroeder last night on ARD.  Lafontaine
sharply attacked the head of the red-green coalition in his book, "The
Heart Beats Left," and suggests Schroeder deceived voters.  The book
will be published in excerpts by the "World on Sunday" paper.

<H1>
Union wants to stop Bundesrat's reform plans
</H1>
The CDU will block reform plans of the federal government in the
Bundesrat.  According to CDU Chairman Schaeuble, the party leadership
is in agreement that the union-led states will refuse the health
reform and savings plans for economic rehabilitation in its present
form.  The CDU leadership agreed with the CSU leader last night.

<H1>
Union parties work out savings program
</H1>
The CDU and CSU want to reduce their somewhat different approaches to
tax policy by finding a common denominator.  The goal of the meeting
is an alternative program to the Federal government's savings package.
The CSU jumped the start with a proposal to reduce the top tax bracket
in two steps through to the year 2003 from 52% to 35%.

<H1>
Greens want separation of mandate and party office
</H1>
The Greens want to abolish the strict separaton of mandate and party
office, but keep the double leadership.  The federal representative of
the Greens supports this reform proposal of the internal party
structure commision.  Council spokesperson Radcke said after the
meeting in Berlin that the federal council doesn't think creating the
post of General Secretary makes sense, as suggested by Foreign
Minister Fischer.  Constitutional changes for the Greens have to be
approved by two-thirds of a federal party convention.  The reform
proposals will be discussed in state councils for the Greens.  This
convention will meet mid-October in Magdeburg.

<H1>
Still 30,000 without apprenticeship position
</H1>
About 30,000 young adults in Germany are still looking for
apprenticeship positions, according to the Frankfurter Rundschau at
the beginning of the education year.  According to the paper's
figures, trade offered 184,000 training positions, and it looks better
in industry and business.  In those areas, 300,000 training contracts
were signed, up by more than 10% from last year.  The increase is
possible due to the government's immediate program.

<H1>
Zwickel criticizes government's savings plans
</H1>
IG-metall Chairman Zwickel has once again criticized the savings plans
of the federal goverment as unfair towords retirees, the unemployed,
and social aid recipients.  An especially high amount is demanded from
these groups, while the wealthy aren't required to pay more, said
Zwickel to BILD.  The unionist promoted a property levy that should
flow into innovation funds for research and development and ecological
projects.

<H1>
Compromise in fight on retirement age
</H1>
Both sides in the fight about retiremtn at age 60, the Federal
Government and IG-Metall, have indicatied readiness to compromise.  At
the IG-Metall congress in Hamburg, Employment Minister Riester said
today he wants to seek talks with IG Metall and retiree insurance
carriers.  IG-Metall Chairman Zwickel spoke of a favorable fundamental
basis for a solution in this conflict.

<H1>
Bank merger falls through
</H1>
A large merger of the Deutsche and Dresdner Banks in personal banking
has fallen through.  Both institutions made known last night that the
proceedings ended without a solid result.  Accrding to the Dresdner
Bank, cooperation for customers, and thus the whole bank, would have
been bound to too many restrictions.

<H1>
France Telecom buys into E-Plus
</H1>
France Telecom has entered into the German market.  The corporation
bought 3.4 billion Marks worth of shares from the Essen-based mobile
communicaions provider E-Plus.  The British mobile communication
company Woderforn Airtouch (sp?) was also in on the deal.  After the
failure of merger plans, the French are going their own way into the
German telephone market.

<H1>
Gas explosion kills 2
</H1>
A gas explosion killed two girls, age 9 and 11, in Monheim, near
Leverkusen, North Rhine-Westphalia.  The girls were taking a bath
while visiting their grandparents when a gas bottle allegedly
exploded.  The grandparents and the girls' father were critically
injured, the grandfather teetering on the brink of life and death.
The house was mostly destroyed; surrounding buildings also sustained
damage.
