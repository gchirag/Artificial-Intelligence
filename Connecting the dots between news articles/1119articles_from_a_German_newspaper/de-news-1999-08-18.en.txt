<H1>
3800 victims of earthquake in Turkey, amongst them some Germans
</H1>
The number of victims killed by the earthquake in Turkey continues to rise.
So far, authorities have counted 3800 dead and almost 18'000 injured. Three
Germans are among the dead. Six other Germans are still being missed.
Meanwhile, more than 1000 helpers from 19 countries work in the disaster
area. The German Forces has also offered Turkey its support. The largest
Turkish refinery in Ismit is still in flames. Specialists try to use water
bombers from Germany and three other countries to control the fire.

<H1>
Drug industry rejects agreement on medicament budget
</H1>
Last night's agreement between Federal Health Minister Angelika Fischer and
registered physicians about the drug budget for the current year has been
partially met with criticism. Representatives of the pharma industry stated
that the industry expects profit losses worth billions of marks.
The physicians said they still wanted to do away with budgeting. Union and
FDP politicians were also opposed to the agreement.
One item of the agreement is that more generic products be prescribed to
stay within the budget.

<H1>
Hospitals demand withdrawal of planned health reform
</H1>
Representatives of hospitals have called on the German government to
withdraw the health reform to be introduced on January 1, 2000. After a
meeting with Health Minister Angelika Fischer the president of the German
Hospital Society, Mr. Pfoehler, said in Bonn that the hospitals were
overburdened. The government had no solutions for the existential problems
of the hospitals.

<H1>
Unions threaten with consequences if shopping hours are extended
</H1>
The Alliance for Employment might suffer if the shopping hours are
extended. The union for retail, banking, and insurance HBV threatened the
government with more conflicts within the Alliance for Employment if the
shopping hours law were relaxed. In a preemptive move, the union for
employees in North Rhine-Westphalia, DAG, has given notice of termination
of the collective agreement. By doing this, the union wants to make sure
that if the shopping hours are extended the wages can also be raised.

<H1>
Shareholders of IG Farben decide compensation for forced labourers
</H1>
Former forced labourers of IG Farben will be compensated with 3 million
marks from a foundation. This was decided today at the stockholders meeting
in Frankfurt on Main. 92.5 percent of the shareholders of the assign of IG
Farben Industrie AG in liquidation voted for the motion of the liquidators.
The money will be taken from the company's assets. Representatives of the
Critical Shareholders and of NS victim associations, however, demanded a
quick liquidation of the company and to pay the full remaining assets of 21
million DM to the victims.

<H1>
Hombach now also criticized by his own party
</H1>
Former Secretary of the Chancellor's Office Bodo Hombach is now also
criticized by his own party for his role in the latest real estate affaire.
The president of the North Rhine-Westphalian SPD, Franz Muentefering, said
it was pivotal that Hombach had allowed himself in the 80s when he was
chief executive officer of the NRW-SPD to co-operate privately with one of
the largest contractors of the party. The current-affairs magazine Stern
had reported yesterday about construction project in Canada in which
Hombach's mother had a share and, indirectly, an advertiser who had
received honorariums of millions of marks for ad campaigns for the NRW-SPD.

<H1>
Budget cuts for Deutsche Welle unavoidable
</H1>
The government sticks to the budget cuts for Deutsche Well, Germany's
international radio station. Today, government spokesman Heye called on the
station to make constructive suggestions. Heye said, one option was closer
collaboration with the public broadcasters ARD and ZDF. He could not follow
statements by Deutsche Welle president Weyrich that the budget cuts would
result in 745 lost jobs.

<H1>
German re-approved for meetings of EU ministers
</H1>
The quarrel between Finland and Germany about language use within the EU
administration has been resolved. German may be used again when EU
ministers meet. For weeks, Finland had insisted on the EU's unwritten law
that only English, French and the presiding country's language may be used
at informal minister meetings. Finland is currently presiding over the EU.
